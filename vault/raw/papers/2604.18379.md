Abstract
Most data-driven ionospheric forecasting models operate on gridded products, which do not preserve the time-varying sampling structure of satellite-based sensing. We instead model the ionosphere as a dynamic graph over ionospheric pierce points (IPPs), with connectivity that evolves as satellite positions change. Because satellite trajectories are predictable, the graph topology over the forecast horizon can be constructed in advance. We exploit this property to condition forecasts on the future graph structure, which we term ephemeris conditioning. This enables prediction on lines of sight that appear only in the forecast horizon. We evaluate our framework on multi-GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite System) data from a co-located receiver pair in Singapore spanning January 2023 through April 2025. The task is to forecast Rate of TEC Index (ROTI)-defined irregularities at 5-minute cadence up to 2 hours ahead as binary probabilistic classification per node. The resulting model, IonoDGNN, achieves a Brier Skill Score (BSS) of 0.49 and a precision-recall area under the curve (PR-AUC) of 0.75, improving over persistence by 35% in BSS and 52% in PR-AUC, with larger gains at longer lead times. Ablations confirm that graph structure and ephemeris conditioning each contribute meaningfully, with conditioning proving essential for satellites that rise during the forecast horizon (receiver operating characteristic AUC: 0.95 vs. 0.52 without). Under simulated coverage dropout, the model retains predictive skill on affected nodes through spatial message passing from observed neighbors. These results suggest that dynamic graph forecasting on evolving lines of sight is a viable alternative to grid-based representations for ionospheric irregularity forecasting. The model and evaluation code will be released upon publication.

Introduction
Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) signals propagate through the ionosphere along satellite–receiver paths, with delays proportional to the integrated electron density, or total electron content (TEC)
[
37
]
. Most data-driven approaches to forecasting TEC
[
32
,
49
,
44
]
rely on gridded ionospheric products
[
19
,
26
,
35
]
. These products provide stable representations of large-scale ionospheric behavior, but they are not direct measurements. Their estimates depend on model assumptions, how sparse observations are turned into grid values, and availability of input observations
[
11
,
28
]
. As a result, they can carry method-dependent biases, with these effects most pronounced at low latitudes and in regions with sparse observation coverage
[
38
,
21
]
.
Such regions also exhibit rapid plasma density variations, forming sharp and localized structures such as equatorial plasma bubbles (EPBs)
[
5
]
. These irregularities disrupt radio signals through scattering and scintillation
[
25
]
, leading to loss of lock and degraded positioning performance
[
2
]
. Forecasting such behavior is a priority for GNSS applications, but it is also challenging; such structures evolve quickly and on small spatial scales, meaning that the gridding, smoothing, and time-averaging used in map data can weaken or distort the relevant features.
Additionally, this representation choice may introduce an overlooked issue for machine learning: models trained on gridded products risk learning the artifacts of the mapping process alongside the physical signal
[
18
]
. The same concern cuts across the existing formulations for irregularity forecasting. Regional-map approaches predict occurrence over spatial grids
[
50
,
4
,
31
]
, providing coverage but feeding the model targets shaped by approximation. There are also single-station approaches that forecast proxy indices such as Rate of TEC Index (ROTI) or amplitude scintillation index (
S
4
S_{4}
) at individual receivers
[
3
,
43
,
9
]
, capturing local dynamics but not producing a regional picture. Both approaches are therefore constrained by data representations.
Recent studies have introduced graph neural networks (GNNs) to ionospheric modeling, enabling spatial message passing between nodes. However, they generally operate on fixed grid nodes
[
12
,
48
,
23
]
or static station graphs
[
22
]
, leaving the underlying representation similar.
This motivates a different viewpoint. The ionosphere is not observed on a fixed grid but through satellite–receiver lines of sight (LoS) whose sampling structure evolves as satellites move. At any instant, GNSS observes the ionosphere at a set of ionospheric pierce points (IPPs) that move, appear, and disappear as satellites rise and set. This evolving LoS structure shapes what is observed, where, and with what spatial coverage. Prior work has modeled ionospheric quantities along individual LoS
[
7
,
34
]
, but not the evolving spatial structure among them. Dynamic GNNs (DGNNs), where the node and edge sets change at each timestep, offer a natural formalism for this structure and have shown promise in domains with similarly transient sensor configurations, including traffic forecasting
[
46
]
and weather prediction
[
41
]
. We apply this perspective to GNSS in this study.
Our model, IonoDGNN (Ionospheric DGNN), operates directly on GNSS LoS observations where each node is an IPP carrying observation and ephemeris-derived features, and edges encode spatial relationships among simultaneous IPPs without imposing a fixed grid. In this representation, missing data is naturally absent rather than imputed, and the model adapts to the transient observation density at each timestep, learning from only the observed signal without inheriting biases from mapping approximations.
Our key novelty is what we term
ephemeris conditioning
, which leverages the near-deterministic nature of satellite orbits. Since broadcast orbit products are available up to 24 hours ahead with sub-meter accuracy
[
20
]
, the full forecast-horizon graph can be constructed in advance. This means future IPP locations, their graph connectivity, and associated node and edge features are all computable from ephemeris and time information. This extends the principle of conditioning on known future scalars
[
30
]
to the full GNSS observation graph. The model can therefore reason about where the ionosphere will be sampled over the forecast window, which is critical when new satellites regularly enter the observation set within the forecast horizon. It also enables richer modeling, as the sampling structure itself may carry predictive information.
As a proof of concept, we apply the proposed approach to forecasting ROTI-defined irregularities at 5-minute cadence up to 2 hours ahead as binary per-node event prediction from sparse multi-GNSS observations. The data configuration uses a zero-baseline receiver pair in Southeast Asia. Since labeling irregularity events from raw GNSS data is challenging, we take advantage of this co-location to cross-validate detected events, yielding consistent labels for training and evaluation under realistic uncertainty.
We test with emphasis on event-focused verification. Prior ROTI and scintillation forecasting studies primarily report metrics dominated by the quiet-time majority (e.g., RMSE, R
2
, accuracy), which can obscure performance for imbalanced datasets
[
3
,
31
,
43
]
. Event-focused evaluation, probabilistic skill metrics and broader contingency-based verification partly exist but remain sparse across the literature
[
10
,
8
,
50
]
. Accordingly, we adopt a broader metric set aimed at event detection skill. Evaluation includes ablations isolating the contributions of graph representation and ephemeris conditioning, a persistence baseline for comparison, and an analysis of prediction on nodes with missing observations under simulated coverage dropout.
In summary, this paper makes two main contributions:
1.
We introduce the first dynamic graph formulation for ionospheric modelling that represents GNSS LoS observations as evolving graph nodes without intermediate griding.
2.
We propose ephemeris conditioning, enabling better overall performance and generalization to LoS from rising satellites in the forecast horizon by leveraging the future graph topology constructed from satellite ephemeris.

2.3

2.4
Data Split
The dataset is split into three chronological subsets with 2-hour gaps between splits to prevent information leakage: training (March 2023 to August 2024), validation (September to December 2024), and test (December 2024 to April 2025). Timesteps containing only unverified or invalid segments, with no confirmed events on any satellite, are excluded from training and validation to control training noise. When confirmed and ambiguous labels coexist at the same timestep across different satellites, the timestep is retained, but unverified and invalid IPPs are masked from the loss function (see Section

4.4
Prediction Under Coverage Dropout
In operational GNSS networks, receiver outages and data gaps are common. The model already handles variable observation density by design: satellite visibility changes continuously, and training samples range from nominal coverage to as few as 3 active IPPs per snapshot (Section

5
Discussion
5.1
Limitations
This work presents the first application of observation-space dynamic graph modelling to ionospheric forecasting, and several limitations should be acknowledged. The dataset comprises two co-located stations in a single equatorial region, covering approximately two years of the Solar Cycle 25. While this period includes frequent post-sunset irregularity activity, the model has not been exposed to full solar cycle variability. No confirmed events in the dataset coincide with major geomagnetic storm periods, so model performance under storm-driven irregularities remains untested.
The QC labeling scheme requires both stations to be active, making extension of the dataset difficult. The broader Singapore/Southeast Asia region has limited IGS station availability, constraining multi-station validation without dedicated instrumentation such as ionosondes or scintillation monitors.
The QC procedure itself is a limitation. Zero-baseline receiver pairs are uncommon in operational networks, and the labeling is imperfect: the unverified category likely contains a mixture of genuine events and quiet intervals, while the invalid category may include physical events that manifest on only one receiver due to local multipath or hardware differences. The model’s ability to distinguish these cases through spatial coherence (Section
4.5
) is encouraging, but the lack of independent ground truth limits full characterization of these ambiguous samples.
Addressing these limitations will require expanding the dataset to include more stations, longer time periods, and complementary observation types for independent validation. The model architecture itself is flexible enough to accommodate these extensions, but their implementation and evaluation remain future work.
Lastly, our current implementation uses precise orbits, not broadcast orbit products. This should make little difference as the short-time prediction horizon is nearly deterministic. However, operational usage still requires further demonstration.
5.2
Broader Implications and Future Directions
A key implication of this work concerns the role of data representation in ionospheric forecasting. The results indicate that operating directly in GNSS observation space enables the model to exploit the native LoS sampling structure without requiring gridding or spatial smoothing. In this formulation, spatial relationships among simultaneous observations are explicitly modeled, and the consistent gains of the graph-based variants, together with the behavior on ambiguous labels (Section
4.5
) and the spatial recovery under coverage dropout (Section
4.4
), indicate that these relationships carry meaningful information for irregularity forecasting.
Retaining the native LoS structure also enables conditioning on the known future sampling structure. Through ephemeris conditioning, the model incorporates where and how the ionosphere will be sampled in future steps. The strong degradation observed without conditioning, particularly for newly appearing nodes, highlights a new prediction capability tied to GNSS.
A further consequence of operating on native observations using graphs is the ability to predict on nodes whose history becomes unavailable due to coverage gaps. The dropout analysis (Section
4.4
) shows that the graph attention mechanism transfers information from observed neighbors, maintaining meaningful predictions on affected nodes without external interpolation. Unlike gridded approaches, where gap-filling is a separate preprocessing step decoupled from prediction, the graph formulation performs spatial recovery and forecasting jointly within the same learned architecture. Together, these results suggest that representation plays a non-trivial role in model performance, although isolating its impact relative to gridded approaches requires direct comparison against equivalent grid-based baselines.
Beyond representation, the dynamic graph framework opens several directions for further research. The graph topology itself is a largely unexplored design space: the current
K
K
-NN construction is purely geometric, whereas edges could encode physical priors such as magnetic field line connectivity, reflecting the field-aligned structure of ionospheric irregularities
[
5
]
. Node features could likewise be augmented with additional physical drivers such as vertical plasma drift associated with pre-reversal enhancement
[
1
]
. Because historical and ephemeris-conditioning graphs can be constructed separately, such physics-informed modifications remain fully compatible with the conditioning mechanism.
Scaling to larger and more diverse observation networks is an immediate next step and introduces considerations along two axes. The first is size: as more stations are added, the graph becomes denser and spans a broader spatial extent, which can help capture the regional evolution of irregularities. The number of nodes and edges also grows, raising the computational cost of training. Dynamic graph frameworks such as PyTorch Geometric Temporal
[
39
]
and TGM
[
13
]
provide mechanisms for handling large spatiotemporal graphs, and comparable approaches have been used on graphs with substantially larger node counts
[
33
]
. The second axis concerns the mix of instruments. Different ionospheric sensors operate at different cadences, spatial resolutions, and coverage patterns. A heterogeneous graph
[
41
]
with distinct node and edge types lets each sensor retain its native sampling, rather than being forced onto a common grid through resampling. In this setting, measurement availability becomes part of the learning problem itself, and GNSS can in principle be combined with other ionospheric sensing systems in a single framework, though doing so demands a complex graph design and careful attention to data consistency across sensors.
The predictions on ambiguous labels (Section
4.5
) suggest that the model implicitly learns aspects of data consistency through spatial coherence: when an anomalous signal appears on a single IPP while its neighbors remain quiet, the model assigns low event probability without explicit quality flags. In a denser graph this property could complement existing quality control by providing a continuous, learned assessment of data reliability alongside the forecast itself, particularly in the cases where zero-baseline cross-validation of the kind used in this study is not feasible.
The proposed formulation is not tied to a specific choice of ionospheric observable. In the current implementation, ROTI is used as the primary input, but the graph construction and model architecture depend only on the LoS configuration and sampling schedule. As a result, other GNSS-derived quantities, such as scintillation indices (S4,
σ
ϕ
\sigma_{\phi}
), calibrated STEC, or VTEC, can be incorporated by redefining the node features without modifying the underlying framework. This reflects a separation between how observations are represented (through the dynamic graph) and what physical quantity is being predicted. The extent to which performance transfers across different observables depends on their statistical properties and noise characteristics, and requires further investigation.
Finally, ephemeris conditioning as a concept is not specific to ionospheric applications. It relies on the availability of known future sampling locations, a property shared by several remote sensing systems such as satellite altimetry, SAR interferometry, and radio occultation. Whether this leads to similar gains depends on the characteristics of each domain but can be a promising way to enhance forecasting skill in other geophysical contexts where sampling density changes and the prediction target exhibits spatial relationships.
6
Conclusion
In this work, we propose a dynamic graph neural network for ionospheric irregularity forecasting that models evolving GNSS LoS structure rather than training on gridded approximations. We also introduce ephemeris conditioning, using known future satellite positions to construct forecast-horizon graphs before observations are made. The model forecasts ROTI-defined irregularities at 5-minute cadence up to 2 hours ahead under a
∼
\sim
7% climatological event rate.
The resulting model, IonoDGNN, achieves a Brier Skill Score of 0.49 and PR-AUC of 0.75, representing 35% and 52% relative improvements over the persistence baseline, respectively. The advantage widens with lead time: at the 2-hour horizon, IonoDGNN maintains BSS
≈
\approx
0.35 while persistence degrades to near-zero skill.
Graph message passing contributes a 22% improvement in BSS and 15% in PR-AUC over the no-graph ablation, confirming that spatial context through the
K
K
-NN topology provides meaningful information beyond what temporal attention alone can capture. Ephemeris conditioning contributes 15% in BSS and 14% in PR-AUC relative to the variant without it, and is essential for new nodes: the full model achieves ROC-AUC 0.95 on IPPs absent from the history window, compared to 0.52 without conditioning.
The model degrades gracefully under simulated coverage dropout, maintaining useful skill on affected nodes through spatial message passing from observed neighbors. Analysis of ambiguous labels shows that the model does not exploit the QC labeling scheme and instead relies on spatial context to identify data inconsistencies.
These results demonstrate that treating GNSS observations as a dynamic graph with known future graph topology is a viable approach to ionospheric forecasting. The framework places no constraint on the choice of observable, and can in principle accommodate heterogeneous observation types and physics-informed graph construction, though these extensions remain to be demonstrated. The dataset, dynamic graph pipeline, and model will be made publicly available to facilitate further research in this direction.
