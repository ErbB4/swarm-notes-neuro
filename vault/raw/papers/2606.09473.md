What Works, What Does Not, and Where
The results assemble into a compact regime map. At short horizons the random-walk floor is near the top of the field ŌĆö beating the NPTS family and CSP and beaten only by the adaptive-online methods (by a bounded relative margin) ŌĆö and the seasonal floor is weak. Limitations and Scope
Not a SOTA claim.
We do not claim a new best forecaster. In the multi-step seasonal regime the floors lose to CSP and only match DeepNPTS; that boundary is reported, not hidden. The win over CSP at one step (
71
%
71\%
) is CSP evaluated outside its multi-step seasonal design regime ŌĆö CSP is top of its own six-dataset suite (Table
2
), and the one-step result is not evidence against CSP but against omitting the floor.
Two corpora.
The regime map is read across two benchmarks (the
2
,
217
2{,}217
-series one-step corpus and the six-dataset multi-step suite) rather than one factorial design; the horizon sweep (Section
5.3
) partly closes this by varying the horizon within one suite.
Construction.
The floors treat horizons independently within a predictive sample, so columns are not coherent trajectories; the one-step interval does not widen with
h
h
(which is precisely the failure ConformalNaive+ routes around); and residual exchangeability holds only approximately under dependence, so coverage is empirical, not guaranteed.
Comparator scope and protocol.
All comparators run at default settings in one shared pipeline; we neither tuned them per dataset nor reimplemented them. The result should therefore be read as ŌĆ£these methods, at standard settings, do not clear the floor at one step,ŌĆØ not as a statement about their best-tuned performance. The DeepNPTS calibration result is for the audited configuration of the companion CSP study (Appendix
A
) and characterises that configuration, not a fully re-tuned DeepNPTS.
Application scope.
The study is univariate and covers short-horizon online and multi-step seasonal forecasting. It does not address covariates, hierarchical or intermittent demand, multivariate trajectories, cold-start series, or long-horizon non-seasonal forecasting, where the floorŌĆÖs standing is untested; the titleŌĆÖs ŌĆ£mandatory baselineŌĆØ claim is intended for the probabilistic time-series settings studied here.
Scores and robustness, left to future work.
The floors use a symmetric absolute-residual score; asymmetric or scale-robust (MAD-studentised) variants may fit skewed or heteroskedastic series better. We did not sweep
╬▒
\alpha

ConformalNaive+ uses the median in-sample error for outlier-robustness; mean-based, rolling, or per-series selection variants are untested and could narrow the gap to the per-window oracle.

It is not state of the art: at multi-step seasonal horizons it is the weakest method, and the seasonal pool (CSP) wins. The contribution is therefore one of benchmarking discipline rather than a leaderboard: this floor, and its seasonal and horizon-adaptive variants, must be reported whenever a learned probabilistic forecaster claims gains, and probabilistic-forecasting benchmarks should report empirical coverage at the nominal level alongside any sharpness score.
