title: Identifiability of Ecological HOIs
---

**Background:** Higher-order interactions (HOIs) are essential for ecological dynamics, yet they often exhibit structural non-identifiability where they can be masked by effective pairwise models fitted to time-series data. This fundamental limit complicates the inference of true underlying ecological mechanisms from abundance patterns alone.

**Question / Future Work:** A major unresolved challenge is to determine the theoretical and practical conditions under which true higher-order interactions can be rigorously identified or, conversely, reduced to effective pairwise descriptions without losing biological explanatory power. Future research is required to integrate independent ecological information—such as life-history traits, phenological data, and biologically justified non-additive terms—with time-series abundance data to move beyond purely predictive models towards mechanistic explanations that are empirically verifiable.

**Why It Matters:** This issue is critical because it addresses the core problem of model identification in complex systems; relying solely on predictive power from time-series fits can lead to fundamentally incorrect conclusions about species interactions, which has profound implications for conservation, management, and understanding ecological stability.

**Evidence:** Our results highlight the need to complement time-series data with additional ecological information to infer interaction structure reliably.
