title: Unobservable Seismicity Rate Estimation
---

**Background:** In the context of subsurface fluid injection and extraction, the seismicity rate (SR)—defined as the expected number of seismic events per unit time—is a fundamental variable for managing induced seismic risks. However, SR cannot be measured directly through physical instruments and must instead be estimated from observed earthquake catalogs.

**Question / Future Work:** A significant challenge in the practical application of feedback control for induced seismicity is that the seismicity rate (SR) is not an directly observable quantity. Future work is required to develop and validate more accurate, robust, and real-time estimation techniques for SR from sparse and noisy seismic event data, as the performance of control systems is heavily dependent on the quality of this estimated signal.

**Why It Matters:** The inability to measure the control variable (SR) directly constitutes a primary bottleneck in implementing closed-loop seismic risk mitigation in real-world reservoirs. Robust estimation is essential for the reliability of any feedback controller.

**Evidence:** First and most important, SR is not a directly measurable quantity in practice.
