# Europe and the Geopolitics of AGI: The Need for a Preparedness Plan

**Authors**: Maximilian Negele, Daan Juijn, Afek Shamir, David Janků, Bengüsu Özcan, Lisa Soder, Lucia Velasco, Max Reddel, Michiel Bakker, Lorenzo Pacchiardi, Maksym Andriushchenko
**Date**: 2026-05-13
**Paper ID**: [openalex:2605.13634](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13634)

## Summary

This paper provides a strategic assessment of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) and its geopolitical implications for Europe. By synthesizing current AI development trends and expert forecasts, the authors estimate a likely AGI emergence window between 2030 and 2040. The analysis highlights significant vulnerabilities in Europe's strategic infrastructure and policy coordination, ultimately arguing for a unified AGI preparedness agenda to ensure regional stability and competitiveness.

## Key Contributions

- Synthesizes empirical trends and expert forecasting to estimate a plausible AGI emergence window of 2030–2040.
- Analyzes how AGI could reshape global economic and military power dynamics, potentially straining current international governance.
- Identifies critical systemic gaps in Europe's AI readiness, including compute infrastructure, talent retention, and fragmented policy governance.
- Proposes a comprehensive European preparedness agenda focused on institutional situational awareness and AI value chain strengthening.

### Approved Open Questions
- Absence of Unified AGI Definition: Without a shared definition, policy makers cannot consistently evaluate progress, calibrate risk, or align on international governance frameworks, which is crucial for preparing for a potential AGI transition.
- Nascent State of AI Safety Evaluation Science: Technical progress in AI capability is currently outpacing progress in the science of safety, creating a critical vulnerability where powerful systems could potentially bypass existing safeguards or act in ways that are fundamentally unaligned with human interests.
